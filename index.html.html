Enterprise Context
How Hospitals Actually Make Decisions

The Reality

Most enterprise hospital purchases involve 5–12 stakeholders, require capital committee approval, pass through IT/security review, and take 6–18 months. Decisions are consensus-driven and risk-sensitive.

Where Glycemic Lives

Glycemic management intersects patient safety, nursing workflows, pharmacy oversight, quality metrics, CMS reporting, and financial performance. This is a system-level decision, not a departmental purchase.

Enterprise Sales Reality

A strong demo does not close enterprise deals. Champions matter more than interest. Clinical validation precedes financial approval. Urgency must be discovered, not assumed.

30-Second Compliant Opener
"Thank you for the time. Glytec's Glucommander provides glycemic management recommendations to assist clinicians in decision-making.

In peer-reviewed studies, hospitals using eGMS have observed reductions in severe hypoglycemia and improvements in glycemic consistency and length of stay.

Discovery Questions by Phase

  • 01
    How are insulin protocols managed today?
    Opens the door to protocol standardization. Listen for "we have a standing order set" — that's your DIY signal.
  • 02
    Is performance standardized across shifts and units?
    The night shift vs. day shift variability is a major pain point. Nursing leadership feels this acutely.
  • 03
    How do you extract and analyze glycemic data system-wide?
    Most hospitals cannot answer this confidently. That's the gap Command Center addresses.
  • 04
    Is reporting automated or manual?
    Manual reporting = IT burden + delay + human error. Sets up the efficiency story.
  • 01
    Where do you see the most inconsistency in glycemic management?
    Lets them self-identify the problem rather than you diagnosing it for them. More credible.
  • 02
    How visible are severe hypoglycemia events at the executive level?
    If the answer is "not very," that's a governance gap. CNO and CQO fear this exposure.
  • 03
    What happens operationally after a glycemic adverse event?
    Root cause analysis, incident reporting, potential regulatory scrutiny. The cost of inaction becomes visible here.
  • 04
    How dependent is performance on individual clinician judgment?
    High dependence = high variability. This is the patient safety argument — not the technology argument.
  • 01
    How do you quantify the cost impact of glycemic variability?
    Most hospitals haven't. This question alone creates value — it forces them to think about a cost they're absorbing invisibly.
  • 02
    Are LOS impacts for insulin-managed patients tracked separately?
    If not, you can offer to help them model it. "Published research suggests severe hypoglycemic events can carry significant cost impact. How is that measured here?"
  • 03
    What happens if outcomes remain flat next year?
    Urgency question. CMS exposure, Star Ratings, payer mix — the status quo has a cost too.
  • 01
    Who owns glycemic governance in this organization?
    Identifies your clinical champion. If no one answers confidently — that's the gap.
  • 02
    Who would evaluate clinical evidence for a decision like this?
    Routes you to the CMO or CMIO. Avoid single-threaded conversations.
  • 03
    Does finance evaluate LOS implications for clinical decisions?
    Gets the CFO in scope. If yes — you need to bring the financial model.
  • 04
    When is IT typically involved in decisions like this?
    Sets expectations for integration timeline and IT approval gate. Don't let IT be a surprise late-stage blocker.

Defining Intelligent Glycemic Management
Official Definition — Use This Language
Intelligent Glycemic Management is a high-reliability, safety-first model designed to give clinicians real-time, FDA-cleared support so insulin decisions are safer, more consistent, and easier to defend across the hospital. It does not replace clinical judgment — it standardizes and supports it.

Three Pillars of IGM

1. Systemwide — every patient, every unit, every shift, no gaps.
2. FDA-cleared, clinically validated insulin dosing logic — not a rules-based calculator.
3. Consistent, validated dosing support that reduces variability and helps clinicians deliver safer, more reliable care.

What IGM Is Not

Not an additional tool clinicians must manage. Not autonomous dosing. A system-level approach that replaces manual, variable workflows. Reduces burden — does not add to it.

SOP-34 v2.0 — Effective Feb 27 2026 — Two Products, Two Different Rules
Glucommander is an FDA-cleared Class II medical device. Command Center is non-device clinical decision support software (21 USC 360j(o)(1)(E)). They have different compliance rules and you must never conflate them.

Key principle: FDA regulates functions, not products. Every claim you make about Glucommander or Command Center defines its intended use. If you claim a function provides patient-specific treatment recommendations, you have made it a medical device — whether or not it is one.
